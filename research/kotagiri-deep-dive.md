## PART A — CORE KOTAGIRI & IMMEDIATE SURROUNDINGS

### 1. Hidden & lesser-known viewpoints, falls & spots

**Rangaswamy Peak & Rangaswamy Pillar**
Two distinct, sacred landmarks revered by the indigenous communities of the Nilgiris (notably the Irula and other hill tribes). The **peak** is a conical sacred hill; the **pillar** is a striking, solitary rock column that serves as a dramatic natural vantage point. Both are lesser-visited and reached via guided forest treks. Culturally and religiously significant — approached respectfully as pilgrimage sites, not just photo stops.

**Kodanad Viewpoint** *(signature, but worth the detail)*
Arguably the most impressive valley viewpoint in the entire Nilgiris — a roughly 220° panorama taking in Rangaswamy Peak & Pillar, Dolphin's Nose (Coonoor), the line of Catherine Falls, the Bhavanisagar reservoir far below, and the Deccan Plateau. Best at clear early mornings before cloud builds.

**Catherine Falls (via Aravenu)**
~8 km from Kotagiri near Aravenu; fed by the Kallar River, cascading ~250 ft (two-tier) amid tea gardens and forest. The trek down is moderately challenging. Views extend toward Dolphin's Nose. Best Oct–May, morning to noon; avoid monsoon/evening descents and unofficial shortcuts.

**Elk Falls & the Echoing Valley of Kookal**
~7 km from Kotagiri, below **Uyilathi village**. Quieter, more intimate than Catherine. From here one can take in the "Echoing Valley" toward **Kookal** — a genuinely off-beat pocket.

### 2. Sholas & ecology

**What a shola is**
Sholas are patches of stunted tropical montane evergreen rainforest that sit in the *valleys and folds* of the high Western Ghats, surrounded by natural rolling **grasslands** — together forming the "shola–grassland mosaic." They have two–three wood strata and are rich in epiphytes (moss, orchids, ferns). Critically, sholas act as natural sponges: marshes capture rainwater and release it slowly through perennial streams, sustaining the region's hydrology.

**Longwood Shola (Kotagiri's crown jewel)**
- The **last and only urban shola** in the Nilgiris — a ~**116-hectare** forest-grassland patch in the heart of Kotagiri town.
- A **Reserve Forest** and a designated **Ramsar site** (wetland of international importance) — a key water source for surrounding villages.
- **Endemic richness:** ~40 tree species, 4 reptiles, 14 frogs and 14 birds endemic to the Western Ghats occur here.
- **Flagship birds:** the black-chinned/Nilgiri laughingthrush (*Montecincla cachinnans*), Nilgiri blue robin (*Sholicola major*), and the vulnerable Nilgiri wood-pigeon (*Columba elphinstonii*).
- **Visiting:** short-to-moderate, beginner-friendly guided trail; best Oct–March. A fragile, protected ecosystem — go with a local guide and tread lightly.

**High-elevation shola–grasslands & the Nilgiri pipit**
The open grassland half of the mosaic is itself crucial habitat — e.g. for the endemic, range-restricted **Nilgiri pipit**, which depends on intact high-elevation grassland (threatened by plantation/invasive spread). Conserving grassland, not just forest, matters.

### 3. Treks & trails

> **Important:** Much of the high Nilgiri range is **Reserve Forest** and closed to outsiders without permission. Several routes require a **Forest Department permit and/or a registered local guide**. Always check current access locally before setting out; this listing is for planning, not a substitute for on-the-ground permits.

**Longwood Shola trail** — Short–moderate, beginner-friendly, in-town. Guided loop through canopy and grassland; the easiest "real shola" experience and a top birding walk. *Best Oct–March, early morning.*

**Kodanad / Kookal trek** — Moderate. From Kotagiri town through tea gardens, hamlets and woods to the Kodanad viewpoint; gradual incline that steepens. Rewards with the 220° Moyar/Bhavanisagar panorama. Pairs naturally with a sunrise start.

**St. Catherine / Catherine Falls trek** — Moderate–challenging. Through tea gardens and dense jungle down toward the foot of the falls. For solitude-seekers; slippery in monsoon — go with a guide, avoid evenings.

**Kil Kotagiri trek** — Lesser-known, off-beat. Unmarked paths through dense woods, tea plantations and quiet viewpoints — a "serenity + mild thrill" route. Best done with a local who knows the paths.

**Sullivan Trail (heritage walk)** — Retraces stretches of the historic 1819 route associated with John Sullivan, who opened the Nilgiris; anchored at **Pethakal/Kathakal Bungalow** (Sullivan's restored house, now a museum) in Kannerimukku. A walking-history experience rather than a hard trek; an annual expedition is held (around mid-June).

**Rangaswamy Peak / Pillar trek** — Moderate, guided, sacred site. A forest trek to the conical sacred peak and the solitary rock pillar; approach respectfully.

### 4. Birdwatching

The Nilgiris are a global endemic-bird hotspot, and Kotagiri — quiet, tea-clad, with Longwood Shola in town — is one of the best bases. **~10 of the 16 Western Ghats endemics** have been recorded around Longwood alone.

**Endemic / star species to look for**
- **Nilgiri Sholakili / Nilgiri blue robin** (*Sholicola major*) — endangered; most active in the early hours.
- **Black-chinned / Nilgiri laughingthrush** (*Montecincla cachinnans*) — endemic, endangered; hill tea-garden edges.
- **Nilgiri wood-pigeon** (*Columba elphinstonii*) — vulnerable.
- **White-bellied sholakili / shortwing**, **Nilgiri flycatcher**, **Black-and-orange flycatcher**, **Nilgiri flowerpecker**, **Painted bush quail**, **Tytler's leaf warbler**, plus wintering **Kashmir flycatcher** and **Indian blue robin**.
- High-grassland special: **Nilgiri pipit** (range-restricted; needs intact grassland).

**Best spots around Kotagiri**
- **Longwood Shola** — the headline site: White-bellied shortwing, Nilgiri laughingthrush, Nilgiri wood-pigeon.
- **Kodanad estate road** — bird en route to the viewpoint through tea and shola edge.
- **Tea-garden hill edges** — reliable for the black-chinned laughingthrush.

**eBird hotspots:** *Kotagiri — General Area* and *Kotagiri — Franciscan Brothers Road* (check eBird for live, recent checklists and exact pins).

**Seasons:** Best in the dry, clear months; avoid the wetter June–August and November. Early morning is prime. A local birding guide dramatically improves sightings of the skulking shola endemics.

## PART B — WIDER NILGIRIS (DAY TRIPS FROM KOTAGIRI)

These sit beyond Kotagiri taluk but are reachable as day trips. Kept separate so the "core Kotagiri" experience stays distinct.

**Ellad** — A favourite quiet walk near Kotagiri through beautiful tea-estate paths; low-key, scenic, uncommercial.

**Uyilathi village & the colonial house** — The hamlet beside Elk Falls, known for a striking British-era European-style house set in greenery; pairs with the Echoing Valley of **Kookal**.

**Coonoor (~23 km)** — Quieter than Ooty; tea estates, Sim's Park, Dolphin's Nose, and the **Nilgiri Mountain Railway** (UNESCO World Heritage toy train). A natural day trip; check live train schedules via Indian Railways.

**Pykara (~beyond Ooty, ~longer day)** — Pykara Lake and Falls; serene, photogenic — a full-day outing combined with the Ooty side.

**Sigur Plateau edge / Moyar valley views** — The dramatic drop below Kodanad looks onto the Moyar river and the Sigur plateau (a key elephant corridor between the Western and Eastern Ghats). Best appreciated *from* the Kotagiri viewpoints rather than entered (core wildlife zones are restricted).

> **Note on Mukurthi NP, Kookal lake, and deep-forest spots:** several specialised high-altitude shola–grassland and wildlife areas (e.g. Mukurthi National Park on the Ooty side) require **Forest Department permits** and are not open-access. Treat these as guided, permit-based excursions and verify current rules locally. *(Specific access details to be confirmed before publishing on the site.)*
